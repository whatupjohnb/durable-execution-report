We surveyed 130 engineers across companies of all sizes — from solopreneurs to
organizations with 1,000+ engineering staff — about what they're building, and
how they keep it reliable at scale. We focused entirely on backend, full-stack,
and AI engineers and directors, in order to really focus on those closest to
infrastructure decisions.

Some findings were unsurprising: 68% of respondents are building AI workflows
in production. Other findings were unexpected: Only 19% of those teams are
"very confident" their infrastructure can handle 2–3× their current scale. At
organizations with 500+ engineers and significantly more resource, that number
is 0%.

That confidence gap is the story of this report; what teams are asking to have
solved vs what tooling choices, outcomes, and confidence levels tell us about
what needs to be solved.

This report also specifically focuses on running asynchronous work in
production (the qualifying question to participate), as well as orchestration,
observability, evals, and agent framework tooling — four of the more emergent
layers nested between an AI model and a production system that actually works
reliably, for every user. Here are the highlights:

- **The reliability burden is growing for teams building AI.** 20% of teams
  building AI in production spend up to half of their time on reliability
  work — twice that of non-AI teams — and are more likely to report an
  increase in that burden over the last year.
- **LLM failures are the new #1 cause of production workflow failures.** 56%
  of teams building AI in production cite LLM or external API failures as a
  top cause, displacing infrastructure crashes for the top slot held by teams
  not building AI.
- **Observability is the #1 problem engineers believe still hasn't been
  solved** — true of those building AI and non-AI applications alike.
- **The teams 3× more likely to report high confidence in scaling AI
  workflows** are using three solutions together: structured durable
  execution, orchestration-native observability, and production evals.
